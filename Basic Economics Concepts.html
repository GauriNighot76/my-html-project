1. Introduction to Economics:

   - Review of economics concepts studied in Class XI.

   - Introduction of two main branches of modern economics: Microeconomics and Macroeconomics.

   - 'micro' derives from the Greek word 'Mikros' (small), and 'macro' from 'Makros' (large).

   - Terms coined by Norwegian Economist Ragnar Frisch in 1933.


2. Main Branches of Economics:

   - Microeconomics focuses on small economic units.

   - Macroeconomics focuses on the economy as a whole.


3. Ragnar Anton Kittil Frisch:

   - Norwegian econometrician and economist, co-winner of the first Nobel Prize for Economics in 1969.

   - Pioneered econometrics and coined terms such as microeconomics and macroeconomics.


4. Historical Review of Microeconomics:

   - Origin traced back to Classical Economists: Adam Smith, David Ricardo, and J.S. Mill.

   - Popularized by Neo-Classical Economist Alfred Marshall in 1890.

   - Contributions by other economists like Pigou, Hicks, Samuelson, and Joan Robinson.


5. Historical Review of Macroeconomics:

   - It existed before microeconomics, with early contributions from mercantilists and physiocrats.

   - Classical economists like Smith, Ricardo, and Mill combined micro and macro analysis.

   - After the Great Depression, John Maynard Keynesâ€™ "General Theory" (1936) advanced macroeconomic analysis.


6. Meaning of Microeconomics:

   - Deals with individual units of the national economy (consumers, producers, firms).

   - Focuses on the economic behaviour of these individual units.


7. Definitions of Microeconomics:

   - Maurice Dobb: Microeconomics is a microscopic study of the economy.

   - A.P. Lerner: It examines individual economic units through a microscope.


8. Scope of Microeconomics:

   - Theory of Product Pricing: Price determination by demand and supply market forces.

   - Theory of Factor Pricing: Rewards for factors of production (land, labour, capital, entrepreneur) in terms of rent, wages, interest, and profit.

   - Theory of Economic Welfare: Efficiency in resource allocation, production, and consumption to maximize societal satisfaction.



1. Features of Microeconomics:

   - Study of individual economic units.

   - Known as price theory for determining prices of goods and factors of production.

   - Uses partial equilibrium analysis (isolating individual units).

   - Based on assumptions like ceteris paribus (other things remaining constant).

   - Employs slicing method, dividing the economy into small units for detailed study.

   - Uses marginalism principle (study of changes in variables).

   - Analyzes various market structures (Perfect Competition, Monopoly, etc.).

   - Limited scope, does not address nationwide issues like unemployment and inflation.


2. Importance of Microeconomics:

   - Explains price determination and the functioning of a free market economy.

   - Assists in understanding foreign trade aspects (tariffs, exchange rates).

   - Helps in economic model building and business decisions.

   - Useful for government policy-making (taxation, public expenditure).

   - Forms the basis of welfare economics (optimal resource allocation).


3. Meaning of Macroeconomics:

   - Analyzes the entire economy.

   - Deals with total employment, national income, investment, consumption, savings, price levels, inflation, and trade cycles.


4. Definitions of Macroeconomics:

   - J.L. Hansen: Studies relationships between large aggregates.

   - Carl Shapiro: Deals with the functioning of the entire economy.


5. Scope of Macroeconomics:

   - Theory of Income and Employment: Determines national income and employment levels.

   - Theory of General Price Level and Inflation: Analyzes price level determination and fluctuations.

   - Theory of Growth and Development: Explains causes of underdevelopment and suggests strategies for growth.

   - Macro Theory of Distribution: Examines the shares of rent, wages, interest, and profit in national income.

1. Recall of Basic Concepts:

   - Want A feeling of lack of satisfaction.

   - Wants are unlimited, recurring, and vary by age, gender, seasons, habits, and culture.

   - Utility: The capacity of a commodity to satisfy human wants.


2. Introduction:

   - Explanation of consumer behaviour related to the maximization of satisfaction.

   - Utility analysis helps understand how consumers try to satisfy their wants with available resources.


3. Features of Utility:

   - Relative Concept: Utility varies with time and place (e.g., woollen clothes in winter, sand at a construction site).

   - Subjective Concept: Utility differs from person to person based on preferences and habits (e.g., stethoscope for a doctor).

   - Ethically Neutral Concept: Utility is morally colourless (e.g., a knife for cutting fruits and for harming).

   - Differs from Usefulness: Utility is about satisfying wants, while usefulness indicates the value in use (e.g., liquor has utility for an addict but no usefulness).

   - Differs from Pleasure: Utility may not always bring pleasure (e.g., injection for a patient).

   - Differs from Satisfaction: Utility is the cause of consumption, and satisfaction is the result (e.g., drinking water when thirsty).

   - Hypothetical Measurement: Utility is abstract and cannot be measured numerically.

   - Multi-purpose Utility: A commodity can satisfy multiple wants and be used in various ways (e.g., electricity).

   - Depends on Intensity of Want: More intense wants lead to greater utility (e.g., food for a hungry person).

   - Basis of Demand: Demand arises from the utility of a commodity.


4. Types of Utility:

   - Form Utility: Created by changing the shape or structure of a material (e.g., toys from clay).

   - Place Utility: Increased utility due to a change in place (e.g., woollen clothes in cold regions).

   - Service Utility: Arises from personal services (e.g., doctors, teachers).

   - Knowledge Utility: Derived from knowing how to use a product (e.g., mobile phone functions).

   - Possession Utility: Arises when ownership is transferred (e.g., from seller to buyer).

   - Time Utility: Increased utility due to a change in time of use (e.g., textbooks during exams).


5. Concepts of Utility:

   - Total Utility (TU): Aggregate utility derived from all units of a commodity consumed.

   - Marginal Utility (MU): Additional utility derived from consuming one more unit of a commodity.


6. Relationship between Total Utility and Marginal Utility:

   - As more units are consumed, TU increases at a diminishing rate, while MU decreases.

   - TU reaches a maximum and remains constant when MU is zero (point of satiety).

   - Beyond the point of satiety, TU declines, and MU becomes negative (disutility).


7. Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility:

   - Proposed by Prof. Gossen and detailed by Prof. Alfred Marshall.

   - States that additional benefit from consuming more units of a commodity diminishes as consumption increases.


8. Assumptions of the Law:

   - Rationality: Consumers aim to maximize satisfaction.

   - Cardinal Measurement: Utility can be measured numerically.

   - Homogeneity: All units are identical.

   - Continuity: Consumption occurs in quick succession.

   - Reasonability: Units are of reasonable size.

   - Constancy: Related factors remain constant.

   - Divisibility: Commodity is divisible.

   - Single Want: Commodity satisfies a single want.

   - Table 2.2 shows the diminishing marginal utility with each additional unit of commodity x.

2. Exceptions to the Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility:

   - Hobbies: Marginal utility increases with collection hobbies (e.g., stamps, coins).

   - Miser: Marginal utility of money increases for a miser.

   - Addictions: Marginal utility may appear to increase for addicts (e.g., alcohol).

   - Power: The marginal utility of power increases with more power.

   - Money: The marginal utility of money may never become zero.


3. Criticisms of the Law:

   - Unrealistic assumptions (e.g., homogeneity, continuity).

   - Difficulty in cardinal measurement of utility.

   - Inapplicability to indivisible goods (e.g., refrigerators, cars).

   - MU of money varies, contrary to the law's assumption of constancy.

   - Focus on a single want, ignoring multiple simultaneous wants.


4. Significance of the Law:

   - Helps consumers maximize utility from limited resources.

   - Useful for government policy-making (e.g., progressive taxation).

   - Explains the paradox of values (value-in-use vs. value-in-exchange).

   - Forms the basis of the law of demand (quantity demanded increases as the price falls).


5. Relationship between Marginal Utility and Price:

   - Practical application of the law of diminishing marginal utility.

   - Consumers compare marginal utility in terms of money with the market price.

   - Consumers buy units where MU in terms of money is greater than or equal to the price.


6. Indifference Method:

   - Developed by J.R. Hicks and R.G.D. Allen.

   - Uses ordinal utility instead of cardinal utility.

   - Indifference curves show combinations of two goods providing the same satisfaction level.

 Meaning of Demand

- Ordinary Language: Demand is simply a desire.

- Economic Definition: Demand is a desire backed by willingness and ability to pay. For example, wanting a television without the purchasing power is merely a desire, not a demand.

- Formula: Demand = Desire + Willingness to Purchase + Ability to Pay.


 Demand Curve

- Graphical Representation: The individual demand curve shows the relationship between price (Y-axis) and quantity demanded (X-axis), typically sloping downward, indicating an inverse relationship.


 Market Demand Schedule

- Definition: Total demand from all consumers at a given price over a specific time.

- Representation: A tabular format showing quantities consumers are willing to buy at various prices, obtained by summing individual demands.



 Individual Demand Schedule

- Definition: Quantity demanded by an individual consumer at various prices over time.

- Example: A table showing different quantities of a commodity purchased at different prices, illustrating the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded.


1. Desire vs. Demand: All desires are not demand; demand requires willingness and ability to pay.

2. Expansion vs. Contraction of Demand: Expansion refers to increased quantity demanded at the same price, while contraction refers to decreased quantity demanded at the same price.

3. Increase vs. Decrease in Demand: An increase is due to factors other than price, while a decrease is the opposite.


Demand Curve Characteristics

- Downward Slope: Indicates that as price decreases, quantity demanded increases.

- Determinants of Demand: Include price, income, consumer preferences, and external factors.


Exceptions to the Law of Demand

- Giffen Goods: Demand increases as price increases due to the income effect outweighing the substitution effect.

- Prestige Goods: Higher prices may increase demand due to perceived value.


 Conclusion

- Demand is a complex concept influenced by various factors, and understanding its dynamics is crucial for analyzing consumer behaviour and market trends.



Introduction

- The law of demand indicates an inverse relationship between quantity demanded and price but does not quantify the extent of change. 

- Alfred Marshall introduced the concept of elasticity of demand to explain this quantitative relationship.


Concept of Elasticity of Demand

- Elasticity: Refers to the responsiveness of one variable to changes in another.

- Elasticity of Demand: Measures how much the quantity demanded changes in response to price changes or other factors.


Price Elasticity of Demand

- Defined as the ratio of the percentage change in quantity demanded to the percentage change in price:

  - Formula**: \( Ed = \frac{\% \Delta Q}{\% \Delta P} \)

- Types of Price Elasticity:

  1. Perfectly Elastic Demand (Ed = âˆž): Any price change leads to an infinite change in quantity demanded.

  2. Perfectly Inelastic Demand (Ed = 0): Quantity demanded remains constant regardless of price changes.

  3. Unitary Elastic Demand (Ed = 1): Percentage change in quantity demanded equals percentage change in price.

  4. Relatively Elastic Demand (Ed > 1): Quantity demanded changes more than the price change.

  5. Relatively Inelastic Demand (Ed < 1): Quantity demanded changes less than the price change.


Methods of Measuring Elasticity

- Ratio Method: Uses the formula \( Ed = \frac{\Delta Q/Q}{\Delta P/P} \).

- Point Method: Measures elasticity at a specific point on the demand curve.

- Total Outlay Method: Analyzes total revenue changes about price changes to determine elasticity.


 Factors Influencing Elasticity of Demand

- Nature of Goods: Necessities tend to have inelastic demand, while luxuries are more elastic.

- Availability of Substitutes: More substitutes lead to more elastic demand.

- Proportion of Income: Higher cost items relative to income tend to have more elastic demand.

- Period: Demand elasticity can vary over time; generally, demand is more elastic in the long run.


 Distinctions

1. Relatively Elastic vs. Relatively Inelastic Demand: 

   - Relatively elastic demand shows significant changes with price changes, while relatively inelastic demand shows minimal changes.

2. Perfectly Elastic vs. Perfectly Inelastic Demand: 

   - Perfectly elastic demand reacts infinitely to price changes, while perfectly inelastic demand does not react at all.



Conclusion

- Understanding the elasticity of demand is crucial for analyzing consumer behaviour and making informed economic decisions. It helps businesses and policymakers predict how changes in price or income will affect demand for goods and services.

 Introduction

- Supply is a fundamental economic concept that describes the total amount of a specific good or service available for sale at various prices.


 Key Concepts

- Total Output: Defined as the total quantity of a commodity produced in a given period.

- Stock: The total quantity of a commodity available for sale at a specific time, representing potential supply.

- Supply: The quantity of a commodity that sellers are willing and able to offer for sale at a given price during a certain period.


 Supply Schedule

- A supply schedule is a tabular representation showing the relationship between price and quantity supplied. It indicates that lower prices result in lower quantities supplied and vice versa.


Individual Supply Schedule

- An individual supply schedule lists various quantities a producer is willing to supply at different prices, demonstrating a positive slope (higher prices lead to higher supply).


 Supply Curve

- The individual supply curve graphically represents the supply schedule, showing the relationship between price and quantity supplied.


 Market Supply Schedule

- A market supply schedule aggregates the supply from multiple firms, illustrating total market supply at various price points.


 Determinants of Supply

1. Price of Commodity: Higher prices generally lead to increased supply.

2. State of Technology: Technological advancements can lower production costs, increasing supply.

3. Cost of Production: Rising factor prices can decrease supply.

4. Infrastructural Facilities: Adequate infrastructure supports higher supply levels.

5. Government Policy: Favorable policies can encourage supply.


 Law of Supply

- States that there is a direct relationship between price and quantity supplied, with exceptions for certain goods.


Economic Terms

- Total Cost: The overall cost incurred in production.

- Total Revenue: The total income from sales.

- Marginal Revenue: The additional revenue from selling one more unit.


 Distinctions

- Stock vs. Supply: Stock is the total available quantity, while supply is the quantity offered for sale.

- Expansion vs. Increase in Supply: Expansion refers to a movement along the supply curve due to price changes, while an increase indicates a rightward shift in the supply curve.


Conclusion

- Understanding supply is crucial for analyzing market dynamics, as it directly influences the pricing and availability of goods and services.



1. Market Structures

- Monopoly: 

  - Single seller with no close substitutes.

  - High entry barriers.

  - Price maker with complete control over market supply.

  - Examples: Patents, OPEC.


- Oligopoly: 

  - Few sellers dominate the market.

  - Interdependence among firms.

  - Can lead to price rigidity and collusion.


- Perfect Competition: 

  - Many buyers and sellers with homogeneous products.

  - Free entry and exit from the market.

  - Price takers with perfect knowledge of market conditions.


- Monopolistic Competition: 

  - Many sellers with differentiated products.

  - Freedom of entry and exit.

  - Selling costs incurred to promote products.


 2. Features of Market Structures


- Monopoly Features:

  - Single seller, no close substitutes, barriers to entry, price maker.

  


- Oligopoly Features:

  - Few sellers, interdependence, potential for collusion, product differentiation.


- Perfect Competition Features:

  - Homogeneous products, free entry/exit, perfect knowledge, price uniformity.


3. Price Determination

- Equilibrium Price: 

  - Determined where demand (DD) equals supply (SS).

  - Example: For `300, the quantity demanded and supplied is 3000 kgs.


4. Price Discrimination

- Discriminating Monopoly: 

  - Charges different prices to different consumers for the same product.

  - Examples include varying fees by doctors or different electricity rates.


 5. Economic Terms

- Oligopoly: Market with few sellers.

- Equilibrium Point: Intersection of demand and supply curves.

- Selling Costs: Costs incurred to promote sales.

- Homogeneous Products: Identical products produced by multiple firms.

- Price Discrimination: Charging different prices for the same product.


 1. Definition and Purpose

- Index numbers are statistical measures that represent changes in economic variables over time, allowing for comparison between different periods.


2. Historical Background

- The concept of index numbers dates back to the 17th century, with the first study conducted by William Fleetwood in 1707.


3. Types of Index Numbers

- Price Index Number: Measures changes in price levels.

- Quantity Index Number: Measures changes in quantities produced or consumed.

- Value Index Number: Measures changes in the total value of goods and services.

- Special Purpose Index Number: Tailored for specific economic analyses.


 4. Significance in Economics

- Index numbers are crucial for:

  - Policy-making and economic planning.

  - Analyzing trends and forecasting future economic conditions.

  - Measuring inflation and deflation.

  - Presenting financial data in a comprehensible manner.


5. Construction Steps

- Define Purpose: Determine the objective of the index.

- Select Base Year: Choose a reference year for comparison.

- Choose Items: Identify the commodities or variables to be included.

- Obtain Price Quotes: Collect data on prices and quantities.

- Assign Weights: Determine the importance of each item in the index.

- Select Formulas: Use appropriate formulas for calculation (e.g., Laspeyres, Paasche).


6. Limitations of Index Numbers

- Sampling Errors: Often based on samples, which may not represent the entire population.

- Data Bias: Potential inaccuracies in data collection can lead to biased results.

- Formula Defects: No formula is perfect; each has its own limitations.

- Quality Changes Ignored: Changes in product quality are often not accounted for, affecting reliability.

Overview of Public Finance

Public finance is a critical area of study that examines the income and expenditure of government entities at various levels (central, state, and local). It encompasses the management of public resources, fiscal policies, and the impact of government financial activities on the economy.


Key Components of Public Finance

1. Public Expenditure:

   - Definition: Expenditure incurred by public authorities to meet collective needs and promote welfare.

   - Classification:

     - Revenue Expenditure: Ongoing costs for government operations (e.g., salaries, maintenance).

     - Capital Expenditure: Investments in infrastructure and long-term assets.

     - Developmental Expenditure: Spending aimed at economic growth and social development.

     - Non-Developmental Expenditure: Routine government functions that do not directly contribute to growth.


2. Public Revenue:

   - Sources:

     - Direct Taxes: Taxes paid directly by individuals (e.g., income tax, wealth tax).

     - Indirect Taxes: Taxes levied on goods and services (e.g., sales tax, GST).

     - Non-Tax Revenue: Includes fees, fines, grants, and special assessments.


3. **Public Debt**:

   - Types:

     - Internal Debt: Borrowing within the country, typically in local currency.

     - External Debt: Borrowing from foreign sources, often in foreign currency.

   - Management: Involves strategies to ensure sustainable debt levels and repayment.


4. Fiscal Policy:

   - Definition: Government strategies regarding taxation and spending to influence the economy.

   - Objectives: Stabilize the economy, promote growth, and manage inflation.


5. Financial Administration:

   - Role: Overseeing the collection and allocation of public funds to ensure efficiency and accountability.


Differences Between Public Finance and Private Finance

1. Objectives:

   - Public finance seeks to maximize social welfare.

   - Private finance focuses on individual financial goals.


2. Expenditure Determination:

   - Governments set expenditure priorities based on societal needs.

   - Individuals base their spending on personal income and preferences.


3. Credit Status:

   - Public finance enjoys a higher credit rating due to government backing.

   - Private finance has limited credit options based on personal financial status.


4. Currency Printing:

   - Governments have the authority to print currency.

   - Private individuals do not have this capability.


5. Elasticity:

   - Public finance can adapt to changing economic conditions.

   - Private finance has less flexibility in response to economic shifts.


6. Economic Impact:

   - Public finance significantly influences national economic health.

   - Private finance has a more localized, marginal effect.


 Social Security Context

- The conventional model of social security involves government payments to support individuals in need (e.g., elderly, and disabled).

- This model is based on a demographic structure that does not apply to India, where challenges such as urbanization and increased life expectancy require a shift towards enabling self-sufficiency and institutional support rather than direct financial aid.


Government Budget

- Types:

  - Balanced Budget: Revenues equal expenditures.

  - Surplus Budget: Revenues exceed expenditures, allowing for savings or investment.

  - Deficit Budget: Expenditures exceed revenues, requiring borrowing.

- Importance: A budget is essential for resource allocation, economic planning, and maintaining fiscal discipline.


Principles of Taxation

1. Canon of Convenience: Taxes should be levied in a manner that is convenient for taxpayers to pay.

2. Canon of Economy: The cost of tax collection should be minimal; excessive costs undermine the effectiveness of the tax system.


 Non-Tax Sources of Revenue

- Include various forms of income that do not involve taxation, such as:

  - Fees: Charges for services provided by the government.

  - Fines and Penalties: Charges imposed for violations of laws or regulations.

  - Grants and Donations: Financial contributions from individuals or organizations.

  - Special Assessments: Charges for specific local improvements or services.

   - Shift towards manufactured goods and engineering products.


 Direction of Imports (1990-91 vs. 2015-16)

- OECD: Decreased from 54.0% to 28.8%.

- OPEC: Increased from 16.3% to 23.6%.

- Eastern Europe: Decreased from 7.8% to 1.9%.

- Developing Nations: Increased from 18.6% to 43.2%.

- Others: Increased from 1.4% to 2.5%.


 Key Export and Import Commodities (2015-16)

- Exports: 

  - Engineering goods (23.0%), Petroleum products (11.9%), and Readymade garments (6.9%).

- Imports: 

  - Petroleum, oil and lubricants (21.8%), Electronic goods (10.5%), Pearls, and precious stones (5.3%).


Recent Trends in Exports

- Engineering Goods: Significant growth, accounting for 25% of total exports in 2017-18.

- Petroleum Products: Transitioned to a net exporter since 2001-02.

- Chemicals and Gems: Consistent contributors to export earnings.


 Balance of Payments

- A systematic record of all international economic transactions, indicating the economic relationship between a country and the rest of the world.


Conclusion

- India's foreign trade has shown remarkable growth and diversification, with a notable shift towards manufactured goods and a growing share from developing nations in imports. The balance of payments reflects the country's economic interactions globally.
